San Mateo Science Center HVAC System

Acoustic Analysis Based on ASHRAE RP-526 Guidelines

Reference: Schaffer, M.E. (2005). A Practical Guide to Noise and Vibration Control for HVAC Systems, Second Edition. ASHRAE Research Project RP-526.

Mitigated: Ground equipment (~48 dB(A) at property line)
Unmitigated: Rooftop equipment (estimated 65-70 dB(A) at residences with penalties)

Detailed Problem Analysis (ASHRAE RP-526 Reference)

Problem ‚ë† : No Acoustic Enclosures for Cooling Towers

ASHRAE Reference: Chapter 3, pages 74-81, Figures 3-1 through 3-12

Issue: Cooling towers are dominant low-frequency noise sources (63 Hz fundamental from fan blade pass frequency, water splash noise at 500-2000 Hz). ASHRAE guide shows cooling towers require comprehensive acoustic treatment including:

  • Full enclosures with acoustic louvers (15-20 dB insertion loss)
  • Water basin silencers to control splash noise
  • Wide-chord fan blades for 8-12 dB quieter operation

Consequence: Untreated cooling towers project 63 Hz tonal noise and broadband water noise directly to residences.

Problem ‚ë° : Elevated-to-Elevated Sound Path

ASHRAE Reference: Chapter 1, page 18, Figure 1-15 "Reflected and refracted equipment sound at a building perimeter"

Issue: ASHRAE explicitly addresses rooftop equipment with clear line-of-sight to receiving properties. Ground-level barriers/distance provide minimal attenuation for elevated sources projecting to elevated receivers.

Consequence: Rooftop equipment at 35 ft projects directly to 3rd-floor residences at 30 ft with minimal attenuation over 620-750 ft distance (~6 dB per doubling of distance = only ~16 dB natural attenuation).

Problem ‚ë¢ : No AHU Acoustic Treatment

ASHRAE Reference: Chapter 2, pages 41-43, Figures 2-19 through 2-21

Issue: Air-handling units require discharge duct silencers and proper inlet clearances. ASHRAE shows AHUs generate 125-500 Hz broadband noise plus low-frequency rumble from fan operation.

Consequence: AHU discharge noise projects unattenuated toward residences.

Problem ‚ë£ : No Low-Frequency/Tonal Mitigation

ASHRAE Reference: Appendix A, page 172, Figure A-5 "Frequencies at which various types of HVAC equipment generally control their sound spectra"

Issue: HVAC equipment dominates at low frequencies (31.5-125 Hz) where standard mitigation is least effective and annoyance is highest.

Consequence: Without specific LF treatment (mass-loaded barriers, reactive silencers), low-frequency and tonal content transmits largely uncontrolled.

Problem ‚ë§ : Improper Measurement Location

ASHRAE Reference: Appendix C, pages 191-196 "Measuring HVAC System Noise"

Issue: ASHRAE emphasizes measurements must be taken at "the location where the noise is objectionable"‚Äînot at convenient property-line locations that miss elevated sound paths.

Consequence: Ground-level measurements underestimate actual impact at upper-floor residences by 7-12 dB.

Problem ‚ë• : dB(A)-Only Assessment

ASHRAE Reference: Appendix B, pages 177-189, Figure B-1 "Frequency weighting curves"

Issue: ASHRAE explains dB(A) weighting underestimates low-frequency annoyance by design (31.5 Hz: -40 dB, 63 Hz: -26 dB, 125 Hz: -16 dB). For HVAC noise dominated by 31.5-125 Hz, dB(A) alone is inadequate.

Consequence: dB(A) readings underestimate actual annoyance by 5-8 dB for low-frequency dominant sources.

Problem ‚ë¶ : No Character-Based Penalties

ASHRAE Reference: Appendix B, pages 180-181, RC and RC Mark II charts showing tonal and low-frequency penalties

Issue: ASHRAE documents that tonal and low-frequency noise is significantly more annoying than broadband noise at the same dB(A) level. Modern standards (ANSI S12.9 Part 4) require +5 dB penalties for:

  • Prominent pure tones (63 Hz cooling tower hum)
  • Low-frequency content (when dB(C) - dB(A) > 20 dB)
  • Impulsive character (compressor cycling)

Consequence: Without penalties, assessment underestimates annoyance by 5-10 dB.

ASHRAE RP-526: "A Practical Guide to Noise and Vibration Control for HVAC Systems"

Author: Mark E. Schaffer, PE, Member ASHRAE

Publisher: American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers, Inc. (2005, Second Edition)

ISBN: 978-1-931862-77-6

Key Finding from Preface (p. xv):

"the main theme of this guide has not changed over the past 14 years‚Äîthat is, most HVAC system noise and vibration problems are system problems that are due to the improper selection, design, or installation of the components into a complete system. More careful attention to these factors will greatly reduce the number and severity of noise and vibration complaints."

The San Mateo Science Center installation demonstrates exactly the failure modes documented in ASHRAE RP-526: proper ground-level mitigation combined with complete absence of rooftop equipment treatment, measured using inadequate methodology that systematically underestimates impact.
