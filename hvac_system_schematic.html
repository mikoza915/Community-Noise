Key Points About Industrial HVAC Noise:

  • Cooling Towers: Highest noise source (85-95 dB). Combination of fan noise (60/120 Hz fundamental) and water cascading sounds. Often the primary complaint source.
  • Chillers: Screw or centrifugal compressors generate 78-88 dB with strong low-frequency components (31.5-125 Hz). Vibration can transmit through structure.
  • Air Handlers: Large fans (70-80 dB) with blade pass frequency (BPF) tones. BPF = (number of blades) √ó (RPM/60). Creates distinct tonal character.
  • 24/7 Operation: Life science labs require constant temperature/humidity control and 100% outside air. No "off hours" for noise reduction.
  • Combined Sound Levels: Multiple sources operating simultaneously. Total noise at property line depends on distance, directivity, and barriers.
  • Low-Frequency Dominance: HVAC equipment produces strong low-frequency noise (31.5-250 Hz) which:
    • Travels farther than high frequencies
    • Penetrates walls more effectively
    • Is underestimated by A-weighting (up to -26 dB at 63 Hz)
    • Causes disproportionate annoyance
  • Power Consumption: Typical 200,000 SF life science building: 625-1,250 kW for HVAC (represents 40-60% of total building power).
  • Mitigation Challenges: Equipment typically located on rooftops for space efficiency, but this elevates noise sources above typical barriers and projects sound over longer distances.

San Mateo Science Center Context:

The San Mateo Science Center (Buildings 901 & 951, totaling 262,000 SF) has rooftop HVAC equipment matching this configuration. Residential properties in Harbortown are 620-750 feet away and report continuous low-frequency hum, particularly noticeable at night. The rooftop location provides minimal natural sound attenuation between equipment and residences.
